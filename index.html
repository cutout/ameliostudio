Hypertext experiments and the weird early days of the personal homepage.

Charlottesville, VA is opposite Silicon Valley in every sense, but it was an ideal place to discover the web. Local faculty like Jerome McGann (The Rationale of Hypertext5) were already experimenting headlong with digital publishing. Obscure texts were scanned into new formats, tagged, remixed. There were hypertext workshops for fiction writers. Academics of all people were proving that computers were cool. Nerds were rejoicing in the streets.

Suddenly it was obvious that the web's creative potential could accomodate anyone who was motivated enough. It even scooped up my friends and me, all Luddite unemployable writing majors. Soon I was spending every moment cramming to learn HTML, XML and Photoshop. Postgrads were forgetting about MFAs and launching design shops instead. Not knowing any better, one of them hired me.

Beyond paid projects, it was the culture of the medium itself that motivated me, then and now. I liked the idiosyncratic self-taught nature of the Personal Homepage which proved that not knowing what the hell you're doing makes things interesting. I stayed up every night experimenting with mine, trying to understand the boundaries of a clumsy format that barely existed. Collectively, I imagined an entire generation doing the same all over the world, staying up late together and trying to figure out the new medium, unaware it might end up being their career.

Case Study

Beatport Live

Live is the lastest product on the Beatport roadmap, a streaming platform for DJs to broadcast their live sets.

In 2004, Beatport began as an online store and news hub for electronic music culture. When it was purchased by SFX in 2013, it became part of a larger umbrella that included ticketing, live events and streaming music. As more energy was being placed around large festivals, SFX strategized how to develop tools to deliver the live experience to fans around the world.

In January 2015, I came on board to create a distinctive theme and overall design language for the new product, consistent across streaming channel pages, branded events, DJ dashboards, ads, and a Mac app for OSX. While web production continued, I also started sketching designs for the OSX app. Using native audio and video sources, the app lets DJs stream their live shows, queue upcoming events, and track stats in real time.

Maternity Neighborhood EHR

The flagship app of a groundbreaking healthcare startup, the EHR dashboard is only product of its kind to provide midwives with real-time maternity data.

Maternity Neighborhood was founded by midwives who were seeking more efficient ways to strengthen communication between pregnant women and providers. I worked closely with a midwifery advocate to explore how providers traditionally handled patient intake and the challenge of getting a technology-shy industry to embrace our apps. Case Study

Readability

A popular bookmarklet that grew into a web platform and eventually an iOS app that held the #1 spot in the App Store.

The idea behind Readability was to solve a simple, common problem: an increasingly noisy, distracting web. The original bookmarklet was an Arc90 lab experiment that removed ads and marginal content with a simple click, rendering the article as a clean, simple, single column of text with superior typography.

In 2011, Arc90 employees formed a small team to develop Readability as a full-scale reading platform. After launch, its disruptive profit-sharing model with publishers made the New York Times. As we developed Readability for iOS, it became the first to have web typography supported by Hoefler Frere-Jones.
