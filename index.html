Darren Hoyt is a product designer and web developer. He lives, writes and sometimes1 makes2 music3 in Gowanus, Brooklyn.

With every project I try to connect my interests in music, art and journalism with the duties of a web designer who loves writing code and nonfiction equally. I began at Convoy4, honed client-service skills for years, bent WordPress into weird shapes. Later I took founding creative roles in products like Readability, Kindling, and currently Beatport Live. If I'm researching, experimenting or building, I'm happy.

I first explored web design in Charlottesville, VA, which is opposite Silicon Valley in every sense, but an ideal culture at that time for bridging the gap between technology and humanities. Local UVa faculty like Jerome McGann5 were already experimenting headlong with digital publishing. Obscure texts were scanned into new formats, tagged, remixed. Academics of all people were proving that computers were cool. Nerds were rejoicing in the streets. English majors were postponing grad school and launching design studios.

Case Study

Beatport Live

Live is the lastest product on the Beatport roadmap, a streaming platform for DJs to broadcast their live sets.

In 2004, Beatport began as an online store and news hub for electronic music culture. When it was purchased by SFX in 2013, it became part of a larger umbrella that included ticketing, live events and streaming music. As more energy was being placed around large festivals, SFX strategized how to develop tools to deliver the live experience to fans around the world.

In January 2015, I came on board to create a distinctive theme and overall design language for the new product, consistent across streaming channel pages, branded events, DJ dashboards, ads, and a Mac app for OSX. Maternity Neighborhood EHR

The flagship app of a new breed of healthcare startup, the EHR dashboard is only product of its kind to provide midwives with real-time maternity data.

I worked closely with a midwifery advocate to explore how providers traditionally handled patient intake and the challenge of getting a technology-shy industry to embrace our apps. Case Study

Readability

A popular bookmarklet that grew into a reading platform and eventually a #1 spot in the App Store.

The problem Readability aimed to solve was simple enough: to clean up noisy, distracting web content. The original bookmarklet was an Arc90 lab experiment that removed ads and marginal content with a click, rendering the article as a clean, simple, single column of text with modern typography.

In 2011, Arc90 employees formed a small team to develop Readability as a full-scale reading platform. After launch, its disruptive profit-sharing model with publishers made the New York Times and CNN and started a larger conversation about supporting journalism. Hoefler Frere-Jones agreed for the first time to have their type rendered as a webfont, greatly enhacing the legibilty in the iOS app.
