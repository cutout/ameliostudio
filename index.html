Darren Hoyt is a product designer and web developer. He lives, writes and sometimes1 makes2 music3 in Gowanus, Brooklyn.

Whenever possible, I connect my interests in arts, humanities and subcultures with the duties of a web designer who loves writing code and nonfiction equally. I like making web products that educate, entertain or make life interesting.

My process is a mix of disciplines learned in 15 years of working with development teams. If I'm researching, experimenting or building, I'm happy.

I began at Convoy4, honed client-service skills, led a team, bent WordPress into weird shapes. Later I took founding creative roles in products like Readability, Kindling, and currently Beatport Live.

I love tools and frameworks, but nothing interests me as the culture of the web itself. My goal is to never stop learning new ways to shape and contribute to it.

Beatport Live

Live is the lastest product on the Beatport roadmap, a streaming platform for DJs to broadcast their live sets.

Press

Beatport Will Bring Videos, Curated Playlists to Spotify

via Billboard

In 2004, Beatport began as an online store and news hub for electronic music culture. When it was purchased by SFX in 2013, it became part of a larger umbrella that included ticketing, live events and streaming music. As more energy was being placed around large festivals, SFX strategized how to develop tools to deliver the live experience to fans around the world.

In January 2015, I came on board to create a distinctive theme and overall design language for the new product that would be consistent across many contexts: streaming channel pages, branded events, DJ dashboards, ads, and a Mac app for OSX. Case Study

Readability

A popular bookmarklet that grew into a reading platform and eventually a #1 spot in the App Store.

Press

Apps Alter Reading on the Web

via NYTimes

Readability gives free online news inbox with upcoming Apple apps

via CNN

The problem Readability aimed to solve was simple enough: to clean up noisy, distracting web content. The original bookmarklet was an Arc90 lab experiment that removed ads and marginal content with a click, rendering the article as a clean, simple, single column of text with modern typography.

In 2011, Arc90 employees formed a small team to develop Readability as a full-scale reading platform. After launch, its disruptive profit-sharing model with publishers made the New York Times and CNN and started a larger conversation about supporting journalism. Hoefler Frere-Jones agreed for the first time to have their type rendered as a webfont, greatly enhacing the legibilty in the iOS app.
